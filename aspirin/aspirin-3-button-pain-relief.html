  1. About aspirin for pain relief

    Aspirin is an everyday painkiller for aches and pains such as headache, toothache and period pain. It can also be used to treat colds and 'flu-like' symptoms, and to bring down a fever (more than 38C). It contains the active ingredient acetylsalicylic acid.

    Aspirin is also available combined with other ingredients in some indigestion and cold and flu remedies.

    You can buy most types of aspirin from pharmacies and supermarkets. Some types are only available on prescription.

    It comes as tablets, suppositories (small plugs of medicine that you push gently into your bottom) and as a gel for mouth ulcers and cold sores.

  2. Key facts

    • It's best to take aspirin with or just after eating food. That way, you’ll be less likely to get an upset tummy or stomach ache.
    • Aspirin takes 20-30 minutes to work.
    • Aspirin is generally safe to take in the first 6 months of pregnancy. It's not recommended in the last 3 months of pregnancy or while breastfeeding.
    • Never give aspirin to children under the age of 16 (unless their doctor prescribes it). It can cause serious, even fatal, side effects.
    • Aspirin is also called by the brand names Caprin, Disprin, and Nu-Seals. The brand name for aspirin as a suppository is Resprin. Aspirin as a mouth gel has the brand name Bonjela. Aspirin is an ingredient in Anadin Original, Anadin Extra, Alka-Seltzer Original, Alka-Seltzer XS and Beechams Powders.

  3. Who can and can't take aspirin for pain relief

    Most people aged 16 and over can safely take aspirin.

    However, aspirin isn't suitable for some people.

    Never give aspirin to a child younger than 16. There’s a possible link between aspirin and Reye's syndrome in children. Reye's syndrome is a very rare illness that can cause serious liver and brain damage.

    Never give aspirin to children under 16, unless their doctor prescribes it.

    To make sure aspirin is safe for you, tell your doctor if you have:

    • an allergy to aspirin or similar painkillers such as ibuprofen
    • or have ever had a stomach ulcer
    • high blood pressure
    • indigestion
    • heavy periods
    • recently had a stroke
    • asthma or lung disease
    • ever had a blood clotting problem
    • severe liver or kidney problems
    • gout – it can get worse for some people who take aspirin
    • heavy periods - they can get heavier with aspirin

    Check with your doctor that it’s safe for you to take aspirin if you’re pregnant, trying to get pregnant or if you want to breastfeed.

  4. How and when to take

    The dose of aspirin that’s right for you depends on the kind of aspirin you're taking, why you’re taking it and how well it helps your symptoms.

    Aspirin tablets

    Aspirin usually comes as 300mg tablets.

    The usual dose is 1 or 2 tablets every 4-6 hours.

    Don't take more than 12 tablets in 24 hours. Wait at least 4 hours between doses.

    Aspirin comes as several different types of tablets:

    • standard tablets – that you swallow whole with water
    • soluble tablets – that you dissolve in a glass of water
    • enteric coated tablets – that you swallow whole with water. These tablets have a special coating that means they may be gentler on your stomach. Do not chew or crush them because it will stop the coating working. If you also take indigestion remedies, take them at least 2 hours before or after you take your aspirin. The antacid in the indigestion remedy affects the way the coating on these tablets works.

    You can buy aspirin tablets and soluble tablets from both pharmacies and supermarkets (but there is less choice of pack sizes and brands in supermarkets).

    Enteric coated tablets are prescription only.

    Aspirin suppositories

    Aspirin suppositories are small plugs of medicine that you push gently into your bottom. To use them, follow the instructions on the leaflet inside the packet.

    Aspirin suppositories come in 2 strengths. They contain 150mg or 300mg of aspirin. You can buy them from a pharmacy.

    The usual dose is 3 to 4 suppositories every 4 hours.

    Don't use more than 12 of the 300mg or 24 of the 150mg suppositories in 24 hours. Wait at least 4 hours between doses.

    Aspirin mouth gel

    You can buy aspirin mouth gel from pharmacies and supermarkets.

    For mouth ulcers or sores, massage about a centimetre (half an inch) of gel onto the sore area. Put it on the inside of your mouth or gums every 3 hours as needed.

    If you have dentures (false teeth), take them out before you put on the mouth gel. Then wait at least 30 minutes after applying the gel before putting your dentures back in your mouth.

    What if I take too much?

    Taking 1 or 2 extra tablets by accident is unlikely to be harmful.

    The amount of aspirin that can lead to overdose varies from person to person.

    Contact your doctor straight away if you take more than 1 or 2 extra tablets, or if you get side effects such as:

    • feeling sick
    • ringing in the ears (tinnitus)
    • hearing problems
    • confusion
    • dizziness

    If you take more than 1 or 2 extra aspirin tablets by accident, or if you feel sick, dizzy, confused or you have hearing problems, call your doctor straight away.

  5. Side effects

    Like all medicines, aspirin can cause side effects although not everyone gets them.

    Common side effects

    Common side effects of aspirin happen in more than 1 in 100 people. Talk to your doctor or pharmacist if they bother you or don’t go away:

    • mild indigestion
    • bleeding more easily than normal - for example, nosebleeds and bruises

    Serious side effects

    It happens rarely, but some people have serious side effects after taking aspirin.

    Tell a doctor straight away if you get:

    • reddening of the skin with blisters or peeling, possibly with a high fever and joint pains
    • signs of unusual bleeding, such as coughing up blood, blood in your vomit or pee, or black poo
    • signs of inflammation of the liver (hepatitis), such as yellowing of the skin or whites of the eye, fever, chills, tiredness, loss of appetite, tummy pain and feeling sick
    • signs of high levels of uric acid in the blood, such as pain in the joints of your hands or feet
    • signs of salt or water retention, such as swelling of hands or feet

    Serious allergic reaction

    In rare cases, it’s possible to have a serious allergic reaction to aspirin.

    A serious allergic reaction is an emergency. Contact a doctor straight away if you think you or someone around you is having a serious allergic reaction.

    The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

    • getting a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
    • wheezing
    • tightness in the chest or throat
    • having trouble breathing or talking
    • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat

    These aren’t all the side effects of aspirin. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicines packet.

    You can report any suspected side effect to the UK safety scheme.

  6. How to cope with side effects

    What to do about:

    • mild indigestion – take your aspirin just a few minutes before or after a meal. If the indigestion still doesn’t go away, it could be a sign that the aspirin has caused a stomach ulcer. Talk to your doctor - they may prescribe something to protect your stomach or switch you to a different medicine.
    • bleeding more easily than normal - be careful when doing activities that might cause an injury or a cut. Don't do contact sports such as football, rugby and hockey. Wear gloves when you use sharp objects like scissors, knives, and gardening tools. Use an electric razor instead of wet shaving and use a soft toothbrush and waxed dental floss to clean your teeth. See a doctor if you're worried about any bleeding.

  7. Cautions with other medicines

    Some medicines interfere with the way aspirin works.

    Tell your doctor if you're taking these medicines before you start taking aspirin:

    • medicines to thin blood or prevent blood clots such as clopidogrel and warfarin – taking them with aspirin might cause bleeding problems
    • medicines to prevent organ rejection after a transplant such as ciclosporin and tacrolimus
    • medicines to treat high blood pressure such as furosemide and ramipril
    • digoxin, a medicine for heart problems
    • lithium, a medicine for mental health problems
    • medicines for pain and inflammation such as ibuprofen and prednisolone
    • acetazolamide, for an eye problem called glaucoma
    • methotrexate, a medicine used to treat rheumatoid arthritis
    • diabetes medicines, such as gliclazide

    Mixing aspirin with herbal remedies or supplements

    Aspirin may not mix well with quite a lot of complementary and herbal medicines. Aspirin could change the way they work and increase your chances of side effects.

    For safety, speak to your pharmacist or doctor before taking any herbal or alternative remedies with aspirin.

    Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you are taking any other medicines, including herbal medicines and supplements.

  8. Pregnancy and breastfeeding

    It’s safe to take aspirin as a painkiller for the first 30 weeks of pregnant.

    Don’t take aspirin after 30 weeks of pregnancy. For most women, paracetamol is the best painkiller to take in late pregnancy.

    If you’ve taken aspirin after week 30, especially if you’ve taken it for a long time, tell your doctor or midwife straight away so they can check that your baby is well.

    Breastfeeding and aspirin

    Aspirin isn’t normally recommended during breastfeeding. For most women, it’s better to take paracetamol or ibuprofen to control pain or fever while you are breastfeeding.

    Talk to your doctor if you’re trying to get pregnant, are already pregnant, or you want to breastfeed.

  9. Common questions

    How does aspirin for pain relief work?

    Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid) is a type of medicine known as anti-inflammatory and antiplatelet drug.

    If you've been hurt or have an infection, your body makes hormones called prostaglandins. The prostaglandins cause swelling and sometimes fever and they send pain signals to the brain. These things are part of your body’s natural response to injury. The swelling (inflammation) can help your body heal.

    Aspirin stops your body making prostaglandins and this lowers the pain and reduces swelling and fever.

    When will I feel better?

    You should start to feel better after 20 to 30 minutes.

    Is it safe to take aspirin for a long time?

    It's best to take the lowest dose that works for you for the shortest possible time. That way there's less chance that you'll get unwanted side effects like stomach ache.

    How long should I take aspirin for?

    If you’re taking aspirin for a short-lived pain like toothache or period pain, you may only need to take it for a day or two.

    If your doctor has prescribed aspirin for pain relief, you should take it for the time they recommended.

    If you have bought it from a supermarket or pharmacy and need to use it for more than 3 days, ask your doctor or pharmacist for advice.

    Can I take it with paracetamol and ibuprofen?

    It’s safe to take aspirin with paracetamol.

    But don't take aspirin with ibuprofen. Aspirin and ibuprofen belong to the same group of medicines called non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Taken together, aspirin and paracetamol may increase the chance of you getting side effects like stomach ache.

    Is aspirin better than paracetamol?

    Aspirin is better than paracetamol for some pains such as period pain or migraines. Some people find aspirin better than paracetamol for back pain.

    Paracetamol may be better than aspirin for headaches, toothache, sprains, stomach ache, and nerve pain like sciatica.

    Can I drink alcohol with it?

    Yes, you can drink alcohol while taking aspirin. However, drinking too much alcohol while you’re taking aspirin can irritate your stomach.

    For men and women, the advice is to drink no more than 2 units of alcohol a day. Usually, a standard glass of wine or a can of beer is 2 units.

    Why do some brands of aspirin contain caffeine?

    Caffeine is added to some of the painkillers you can buy from pharmacies.

    Research shows that caffeine (the amount you would get in a mug of coffee) may make painkillers work better for some people who are in a lot of pain.

    There’s caffeine as well as aspirin in these brands:

    • Anadin Original (aspirin and caffeine)
    • Anadin Extra (aspirin, caffeine and paracetamol)
    • Alka-Seltzer XS (aspirin, caffeine, paracetamol and an indigestion remedy)
    • Beechams Powders (aspirin and caffeine).

    Does aspirin cause stomach ulcers?

    Aspirin can cause ulcers in your stomach or gut, especially if you take it for a long time or in big doses.

    Your doctor may tell you not to take aspirin if you have a stomach ulcer, or if you've had one in the past.

    If you're at risk of getting a stomach ulcer and you need a painkiller, take paracetamol instead of aspirin as it’s gentler on your stomach.

    Can aspirin prevent cancer?

    Recent research suggests that aspirin could help to prevent certain types of cancer, for example bowel cancer.

    However, don't take aspirin without discussing it with your doctor. Aspirin can cause serious side effects, such as bleeding and not everyone can take it.

    Speak to your GP about the risks and benefits of taking aspirin if you’re at risk of getting cancer.

    Is there any food or drink I need to avoid?

    You can eat and drink normally while taking aspirin.

    Will it affect my fertility?

    There’s no evidence that aspirin reduces fertility in men or women.

    Will it affect my contraception?

    For women, aspirin won’t affect any contraceptives, including the contraceptive pill and the morning after pill.

    What if aspirin doesn't work?

    If aspirin doesn't work, there are other medicines that you can use to treat pain or inflammation, including:

    • ibuprofen
    • paracetamol
    • co-codamol (a combination of paracetamol and codeine)

    Ibuprofen and ibuprofen-like painkillers are sometimes available as creams or gels that you rub on to the part of your body that's painful.

    Some painkillers are only available on prescription.

    If aspirin doesn't work for you, talk to your doctor or pharmacist about other treatment options that might be more suitable. Your doctor may be able to prescribe a stronger painkiller or recommend another treatment, such as exercise or physiotherapy.
