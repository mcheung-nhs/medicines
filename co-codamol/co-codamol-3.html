  1. About co-codamol

    Co-codamol is a mixture of 2 different painkillers – paracetamol and codeine.

    It’s used to treat short-lasting aches and pains including headaches, muscular pain, migraine and toothache. It can also be used to reduce fever (38C and above).

    It’s usually taken when everyday painkillers, such as aspirin, ibuprofen and paracetamol alone, haven't worked.

    Co-codamol comes in 3 different strengths. You can buy the lowest-strength co-codamol from pharmacies. The higher strengths are only available on prescription.

    This medicine comes as tablets and capsules.

  2. Key facts

    • The most common side effects of co-codamol are constipation, feeling sick and sleepiness.
    • Taking too much co-codamol can be harmful. Don’t be tempted to increase the dose or take a double dose if your pain is very bad.
    • It’s possible to become addicted to the codeine in co-codamol, but this is rare if you're taking it to relieve pain under medical supervision.
    • It’s best not to drink alcohol while taking co-codamol as you’re more likely to get side effects like feeling drowsy or sleepy.
    • Co-codamol is also called by the brand names Panadeine, Paracodol, Codipar, Medocodene, Zapain, Kapake, Solpadol and Tylex.

  3. Who can take co-codamol

    Co-codamol can be taken by adults and children aged 12 and older.

    Do not give co-codamol to children under the age of 12 as it can cause severe breathing problems.

    Co-codamol is not suitable for some people. Tell your doctor before starting the medicine if you:

    • have lung problems or breathing difficulties
    • have a head injury
    • have adrenal gland problems
    • have an illness which causes fits
    • regularly drink more than the maximum recommended amount of alcohol (14 units a week)
    • are pregnant or breastfeeding
    • have liver problems
    • are under-18 and have had your tonsils or adenoids removed because of a sleep problem called obstructive sleep apnoea syndrome

  4. How and when to take

    Co-codamol comes as tablets and capsules.

    Swallow them whole with a drink of water.

    For people who find it difficult to swallow tablets or capsules, co-codamol is also available as soluble tablets that dissolve in water to make a drink.

    You can take co-codamol either before or after food.

    How much to take

    Co-codamol comes in 3 different strengths.

    The amount of codeine it contains can be 8mg, 15mg or 30mg.

    All 3 strengths contains 500mg of paracetamol - the same as in a standard paracetamol tablet or capsule.

    The strength of co-codamol appears as 2 numbers on the packet. For example, the strength may be written as 8/500. This means it contains 8mg of codeine and 500mg of paracetamol.

    You can buy the lowest strength of co-codamol (8/500) from a pharmacy without a prescription. The higher strengths (15/500 and 30/500) are only available on prescription from a doctor.

    How often to take it

    The normal dose for pain in:

    • adults is 2 co-codamol tablets up to 4 times a day. Always leave at least 4 hours between doses. The total maximum dose is 8 co-codamol tablets in 24 hours.
    • teenagers aged 16 to 18 is 1 or 2 tablets up to 4 times a day. Always leave at least 6 hours between doses. The total maximum dose is 8 co-codamol tablets in 24 hours.
    • children aged 12 to 15 is 1 tablet of co-codamol up to 4 times a day. Always leave at least 6 hours between doses. The total maximum dose is 4 co-codamol tablets in 24 hours.

    Overdosing on co-codamol can cause serious side effects. Don’t be tempted to increase the dose or take a double dose if your pain is very bad.

    Do not take co-codamol that you have bought for more than 3 days. If the pain doesn’t improve after 3 days, talk to your doctor.

    The total maximum dose of co-codamol for an adult is 8 tablets in 24 hours. Wait at least 4 hours between doses.

    What if I forget to take it?

    If you forget to take a dose of co-codamol, don’t worry, just take it when you next need pain relief. Then continue as before, taking your doses every 4 to 6 hours if needed.

    Never take 2 doses at the same time. Never take an extra dose to make up for a forgotten one.

    What if I take too much?

    Taking 1 or 2 extra tablets of co-codamol by accident is unlikely to be harmful. If this happens, wait at least 24 hours before you take any more co-codamol.

    Taking more than 1 or 2 extra tablets can cause serious side effects. If you've taken an accidental overdose you may feel very sleepy, sick or dizzy. You may also find it difficult to breathe. In serious cases you can become unconscious and may need emergency treatment in hospital.

    If you feel sleepy, sick, dizzy or find it difficult to breathe, call 111 for advice straight away.

    If you need to go to hospital, take the box or leaflet inside the packet plus any remaining medicine with you.

  5. Taking co-codamol with other painkillers

    It’s safe to take co-codamol with other types of painkiller that don’t contain paracetamol such as ibuprofen and aspirin.

    Do not take co-codamol with paracetamol, or other medicines that contain paracetamol, because of the risk of overdose. This includes some painkillers (tramacet and co-dydramol), migraine remedies and cough and cold remedies (Lemsip and Night Nurse).

    Never give aspirin to a child under the age of 16 (unless prescribed by a doctor).

    Before taking any other medicines, check the label to see whether they contain paracetamol.

  6. Side effects

    Like all medicines, co-codamol can cause side effects although not everyone gets them. Many people have no side effects or only minor ones.

    You are more likely to have side effects if you take the higher strengths of co-codamol.

    Common side effects

    Common side effects happen in more than 1 in every 100 people. Tell your doctor if they bother you or don’t go away. Common side effects include:

    • constipation
    • feeling sick or vomiting
    • feeling sleepy
    • headache

    Serious side effects

    Serious side effects are rare and happen in less than 1 in 1,000 people.

    Tell a doctor straight away if you have:

    • a skin rash
    • difficulty peeing
    • heart problems
    • changes in your eyesight
    • dizziness

    Serious allergic reaction

    In rare cases, it's possible to have a serious allergic reaction to co-codamol.

    A serious allergic reaction is an emergency. Contact a doctor straight away if you think you or someone around you is having a serious allergic reaction.

    The warning signs of a serious allergic reaction are:

    • getting a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
    • wheezing
    • tightness in the chest or throat
    • having trouble breathing or talking
    • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat

    These aren't all the side effects of co-codamol. For a full list, see the leaflet inside your medicines packet.

  7. How to cope with side effects

    What to do about:

    • constipation – eat more high-fibre foods such as fresh fruit and vegetables and cereals. Try to drink several glasses of water or another non-alcoholic liquid each day. If you can, it may also help to do some gentle exercise.
    • feeling sick or vomiting – take co-codamol with or just after a meal or snack. Feelings of sickness should normally wear off after a few days. Talk to your doctor about taking an anti-sickness medicine if it carries on for longer.

    You can report any suspected side effect to a UK safety scheme.

  8. Pregnancy and breastfeeding

    Co-codamol is not generally recommended during pregnancy and while breastfeeding.

    Co-codamol contains paracetamol and codeine. While paracetamol is safe to take in pregnancy, codeine is not.

    In early pregnancy, codeine has been linked to some problems in the unborn baby. If you take codeine at the end of pregnancy there's a risk that your newborn baby may get withdrawal symptoms after birth. It may also cause breathing problems in the baby after birth

    It's not generally recommended for women to take co-codamol while breastfeeding. Small amounts of the codeine in co-codamol passes into breast milk and can cause breathing problems in the baby.

    For some pregnant and breastfeeding women with moderate pain, there may be safer alternatives. Your doctor is the best person to help you decide what's right for you and your baby.

    For more information about how codeine can affect you and your baby during pregnancy see the BUMPS leaflet.

    Tell your doctor if you're pregnant or breastfeeding.

  9. Cautions with other medicines

    Some medicines interfere with the way co-codamol works. And co-codamol can interfere with the way some medicines work. Tell your doctor if you’re taking:

    • sleeping pills or tranquillisers
    • antidepressants – some types don't mix with co-codamol
    • medicines to stop you feeling sick or vomiting such as domperidone or metoclopramide
    • blood-thinning medicines such as warfarin
    • medicines to treat infection, particularly rifampicin or ciprofloxacin
    • epilepsy medicines

    Mixing co-codamol with herbal remedies and supplements

    It's not possible to say that complementary medicines and herbal teas are safe to take with co-codamol. They're not tested in the same way as pharmacy and prescription medicines. They're generally not tested for the effect they have on other medicines.

    Tell your pharmacist or doctor if you're taking any other medicines, including herbal remedies and supplements.

  10. Common questions

    How does co-codamol work?

    Co-codamol contains paracetamol and codeine. These 2 painkillers work in different ways to relieve pain.

    Paracetamol seems to work by blocking chemical messengers in the brain that tell us we have pain. Paracetamol also reduces fever by affecting the chemical messengers in an area of the brain that regulates body temperature.

    Codeine belongs to a group of medicines called opiates. It affects pain receptors in the central nervous system and the brain to block pain signals to the rest of the body. When codeine blocks the pain receptors, there are other unwanted effects - for example slow and shallow breathing. It can also slow down digestion, which is why codeine can cause constipation.

    When will I feel better?

    Co-codamol takes up to an hour to work. It keeps on working for about 5 hours.

    If you’ve taken co-codamol for 3 days and your pain has not gone away then speak to your doctor.

    Is co-codamol addictive?

    Yes, taking co-codamol regularly for a long time can make you addicted to the codeine in it. But in reality, if you're taking co-codamol to relieve pain (rather than using it as a recreational drug) it's very unlikely you will get addicted to it because you're not taking it to get a high.

    If you need to take co-codamol for a long time your body can become tolerant to it. That means you need higher doses to control your pain. This isn't usually a problem when you stop taking co-codamol. Withdrawal symptoms can be prevented by reducing treatment gradually.

    Is it safe to take co-codamol for a long time?

    It's not usually recommended to take co-codamol for longer than 3 days.

    Taking co-codamol regularly for a long time can lead to addiction, which might make you feel restless and irritable when you stop taking the tablets.

    Talk to your doctor if you're worried about addiction.

    Are there other painkillers I can try?

    Yes, there are other painkillers you can try.

    Some painkillers work better than others for certain pains. For example, the best painkiller to ease your headache may not be the best one for your backache.

    Before taking co-codamol, try taking paracetamol to see if that helps your pain. Paracetamol can relieve most types of pain.

    Painkillers known as non-steroidal anti-inflammatory medicines (NSAIDs), such as naproxen and ibuprofen, can reduce inflammation as well as pain. These are good for joint, back and muscular pain. However, NSAIDs aren't suitable for some people. This includes people with stomach ulcers or severe heart, kidney or liver problems. If you want to try NSAIDs, speak to your pharmacist or doctor.

    Can I drink alcohol with it?

    Drinking alcohol while you're taking co-codamol can make you feel more sleepy. It can also increase the risk of serious side effects.

    It's best to stop drinking alcohol during the first few days of treatment until you see how the co-codamol affects you.

    If you feel sleepy with co-codamol, stop drinking alcohol while you are taking it.

    Can I drive or ride a bike with it?

    <p>Co-codamol can make you feel sleepy or dizzy. If this happens to you, don't drive or ride a bike until you feel better.</p>

    Can co-codamol cause weight gain?

    There's no evidence that taking co-codamol will make you put on weight or lose it.

    Is there any food or drink I need to avoid?

    You can eat and drink normally while taking co-codamol.

    Will it affect my contraception?

    Co-codamol doesn't affect any type of contraception, including contraceptive pills or the morning after pill.

    Will it affect my fertility?

    Co-codamol can temporarily reduce fertility in men and women.

    If you take high doses of co-codamol for a long time it can cause a health problem called hypogonadism. Hypogonadism is where the body doesn’t make enough sex hormones. This may make it more difficult for you to conceive.

    If you’re worried about co-codamol and your fertility, talk to your doctor.

    Will recreational drugs affect it?

    If you take recreational drugs, such as cannabis, cocaine and heroin, while you're taking co-codamol, you're more likely to get the serious side effects of the codeine in co-codamol. These include breathing difficulties, heart problems, fits and even going into a coma.

    Some recreational drugs, such as cannabis, also increase common codeine side effects such as sleepiness and dizziness.

    Taking heroin while you're on prescribed co-codamol is especially dangerous. You're more likely to get all the side effects of the codeine in co-codamol, including addiction.
