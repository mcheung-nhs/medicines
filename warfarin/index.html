  1. About warfarin

    Warfarin is a type of medicine known as an anticoagulant. It makes your blood clot more slowly by thinning your blood.

    It’s used to treat people who have had a health problem caused by a blood clot such as:

    • a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) – a blood clot in the leg
    • a pulmonary embolism - a blood clot in the lungs

    It’s also used to prevent blood clots if you’re at high risk of having them in the future. People who are at high risk include those with:

    • a replacement or mechanical heart valve
    • an abnormal heartbeat, known as atrial fibrillation
    • a blood clotting disorder, such as thrombophilia
    • a higher chance of having a blood clot after an operation

    Warfarin is only available on prescription. It comes as tablets and as a liquid that you swallow.

  2. Key facts

    • It's usual to take warfarin once a day, normally in the evening.
    • The main side effect of warfarin is bleeding more easily than normal – such as having nosebleeds, bleeding gums and bruising. This is most likely to happen in the first few weeks of treatment or if you’re unwell.
    • You need to have blood tests at least every 12 weeks while you’re taking warfarin to make sure your dose is right.
    • Expect your warfarin dose to go up or down. This is normal. The dose can depend on lots of different things including what you eat and drink, what other medicines you're taking and if you become unwell.
    • Tell your nurse, doctor or dentist that you're taking warfarin before you have any medical or dental procedures, including vaccinations and routine appointments with the dental hygienist. You may need to stop or reduce your warfarin for a short time beforehand.

  3. Who can or can’t take warfarin

    Warfarin can be taken by adults and children.

    Warfarin isn’t suitable for some people. Tell your doctor if you:

    • have had an allergic reaction to warfarin or any other medicines in the past
    • are trying to get pregnant or you are already pregnant - warfarin can be harmful to the baby
    • have liver or kidney problems
    • have had an infection of the lining of your heart
    • have a health problem which causes bleeding (such as a stomach ulcer) or which makes you bruise easily
    • have high blood pressure
    • are taking the herbal remedy St John’s wort for depression

  4. How and when to take it

    Warfarin is a prescription medicine. It’s very important to take it as your doctor advises.

    How to take it

    Take warfarin once a day, at about the same time.

    It's usual to take warfarin in the evening. This is so that if you need to change the dose after a routine blood test, you can do this the same day rather than waiting until the following morning.

    Warfarin doesn’t usually upset your tummy so you can take it before, during or after a meal.

    How much will I take?

    Warfarin tablets come in 4 different strengths. They're colour-coded to make it easier for you to take the right dose. The strengths and colours are:

    • 0.5mg - white tablet
    • 1mg - brown tablet
    • 3mg - blue tablet
    • 5mg - pink tablet

    Warfarin also comes as a liquid, where 1ml is equal to a 1mg (brown) tablet.

    Warfarin liquid comes with a plastic syringe to help you measure the right amount. The numbers up the side show how much liquid you have in the syringe.

    The usual warfarin dose is 10mg (or 10ml of liquid) a day for the first 2 days. Then 3mg to 9mg (3ml to 9ml) a day after that.

    How is my dose worked out?

    Your dose may be made up of a different combination of coloured tablets. For example, for a dose of 7mg, you could be asked to take:

    • 2 x blue tablets and 1 x brown tablet or
    • 1 x pink tablet and 2 x brown tablets

    Your warfarin dose may change often, especially in the first few weeks of treatment, until your doctor finds the dose that’s right for you.

    Why do I need blood tests?

    The aim of treatment with warfarin is to thin your blood but not stop it clotting completely. Getting this balance right means your dose of warfarin must be carefully monitored.

    You will be monitored with a regular blood test called the international normalised ratio (INR). This blood test measures how long it takes your blood to clot. The longer it takes, the higher your INR.

    The dose of warfarin that you need will depend on your INR. Your doctor will try to keep your INR within a certain target range. The target INR depends on your health problem, but most are 2 to 3 or 2.5 to 3.5 INR units. If the blood test shows your INR is out of the target range, your warfarin dose will be increased or decreased.

    You’ll have INR blood tests at your GP surgery or local hospital’s anticoagulant clinic at least once every 12 weeks to make sure your dose is right for you.

    If your INR is stable, you might only need a blood test once every 8 weeks. If it’s unstable, or you’ve just started on warfarin, you may need to have a blood test every week.

    The yellow book

    When you start taking warfarin you may be given a yellow book about anticoagulants. This explains your treatment. Remember to take your yellow book with you to all of your warfarin appointments.

    Your warfarin dose will be written down in it each time you visit your doctor or anticoagulant clinic.

    You’ll also be given an anticoagulant alert card. Carry this with you all the time. It tells healthcare professionals that you’re taking an anticoagulant. This can be useful for them to know in case of a medical emergency.

    If you need any medical or dental treatment, show your anticoagulant alert card to the nurse, doctor or dentist. This includes before you have vaccinations and routine sessions with the dental hygienist. You may need to stop taking warfarin for a short time, or you may need to change your dose.

    What if I forget to take it?

    If you miss a dose, make a note in your yellow book.

    Take the missed dose as soon as you remember. If you don’t remember until the next day, skip the missed dose and take your normal dose at the usual time.

    Never take more than 1 dose a day.

    If you’re worried, contact your anticoagulant clinic or GP.

    What if I take too much?

    If you take an extra dose of warfarin by accident, call your anticoagulant clinic straight away. You may need to change your next dose of warfarin, or have a blood test.

    If you take more than 1 extra dose of warfarin by accident you’re at risk of serious bleeding.

    If you take more than 1 extra dose of warfarin by accident, call your doctor, anticoagulant clinic, or go to your nearest hospital accident and emergency (A&E) department straight away.

    If you need to go to hospital, take the warfarin packet or leaflet inside it plus any remaining medicine with you. If you have a yellow book, take that too.

  5. Side effects

    While warfarin has enormous benefits, the downside is that it can increase the risk of bleeding. This is because while you're taking warfarin your blood won't clot as easily.

    You’re more likely to get bleeding in the first few weeks of starting treatment and when you're unwell - for instance if you have flu, vomiting or diarrhoea.

    Apart from the risk of bleeding, warfarin is a very safe medicine.

    Common side effects

    Keep taking the medicine, but tell your doctor if the side effects bother you or don't go away:

    • bleeding more easily than normal - for example, having heavier periods, bleeding for longer if you cut yourself, bleeding from your gums after you brush your teeth, and bruising more easily
    • skin rashes
    • hair loss

    Serious side effects

    Occasionally, people get severe bleeding from taking warfarin. There’s often no warning of a serious bleed, but keeping your INR in the target range makes it less likely to happen.

    Stop taking warfarin and tell your doctor straight away if you get:

    • blood in your pee or poo
    • black poo
    • severe bruising or bruises for no reason
    • nosebleeds that last longer than 10 minutes
    • blood in your vomit or coughing up blood
    • severe headaches
    • heavier periods, or any other bleeding from your vagina

    Serious allergic reaction

    In rare cases, it’s possible to have a serious allergic reaction to warfarin.

    A serious allergic reaction is an emergency. Contact a doctor straight away if you think you or someone around you is having a serious allergic reaction.

    The warning signs of an allergic reaction are:

    • developing a skin rash that may include itchy, red, swollen, blistered or peeling skin
    • wheezing
    • tightness in the chest or throat
    • having trouble breathing or talking
    • swelling of the mouth, face, lips, tongue or throat

    These are not all the side effects of warfarin. For a full list see the leaflet inside your medicine packet.

    You can report any suspected side effect to the UK safety scheme

  6. How to cope with side effects

    What to do about:

    • cuts - press on the cut for 10 minutes with a clean cloth
    • nosebleeds - nosebleeds can be frightening but they aren't usually serious. Read about how to stop a nosebleed or watch this video. Call your doctor, anticoagulant clinic or go to your nearest hospital's A&E department if a nosebleed lasts for longer than 10 minutes.
    • bleeding gums - use a soft toothbrush and waxed dental floss to clean your teeth. If you wear dentures (false teeth) or retainers, take them out for a few hours a day to give your gums a rest. Don't wear dentures (false teeth) or retainers that don't fit properly.
    • bruising - bruises may happen more easily than normal and take longer to fade. It might help to speed up healing if you put an ice pack wrapped in a damp towel or cloth over the bruise for 10 minutes at a time several times a day.

    What to do to prevent bleeding:

    While you're taking warfarin be careful when you do activities that might cause an injury or a cut or bruising. It can help to:

    • stop playing contact sports or other activities than can cause a head injury - such as football, rugby, hockey and horse riding
    • wear gloves when you use sharp objects like scissors, knives and gardening tools
    • use an electric razor instead of wet shaving with a blade

  7. Cautions with other medicines

    Many medicines and supplements can interfere with warfarin. This can lead to serious side effects. You might need a blood test to check the other medicine isn’t affecting how your blood clots.

    If you’re taking warfarin, tell your doctor before starting to take these medicines:

    • medicines for heart problems such as amiodarone, quinidine and propafenone
    • non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) for example ibuprofen and aspirin
    • cholesterol-lowering medicines such as bezafibrate, gemfibrozil, clofibrate and cholestyramine
    • antibiotics, such as erythromycin, co-trimoxazole or norfloxacin
    • vitamin K supplements
    • miconazole gel for fungal infections like thrush

    Can I take warfarin with everyday painkillers?

    It's safe to take paracetamol while you’re on warfarin. But don't take more than 2g (4 tablets) a day for more than a few days. Taking more paracetamol than this may make your blood slower at clotting. This puts you at risk of bleeding.

    Do not take aspirin and ibuprofen while you’re taking warfarin unless a doctor has said it’s OK to. They increase the chance of bleeding.

    Mixing warfarin with herbal remedies and supplements

    Do not take St John's wort, the herbal remedy for depression, while you’re taking warfarin. It can increase your risk of side effects.​

    Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you're taking any other medicines, including herbal remedies, vitamins or supplements.

  8. Pregnancy and breastfeeding

    Warfarin isn’t normally recommended during pregnancy. It can be harmful to the baby, particularly during the first 12 weeks of pregnancy.

    Warfarin is generally safe to take while breastfeeding.

    For safety, tell your doctor if you're trying to get pregnant, become pregnant or if you are breastfeeding.

  9. Common questions

    How does warfarin work?

    Warfarin is a type of medicine known as an anticoagulant. It makes your blood clot more slowly.

    Blood clotting (thickening) is a complicated process involving substances called clotting factors.

    Clotting factors are made by the liver and help stop bleeding. They work with blood cells called platelets that trigger the clotting process to make sure your blood clots properly.

    To make some of the clotting factors, the liver needs a good supply of vitamin K.

    Warfarin blocks the liver from using vitamin K to make clotting factors. This thins your blood so it takes longer to clot.

    How long does it take to work?

    Warfarin takes about 3 days to build up its blood thinning effect.

    But it can take weeks or even months before you settle on the right dose. This is because everyone reacts differently to warfarin so it's usual for the dose to go up and down when you first start taking it.

    You won’t feel any different while you take warfarin. The only way to check it’s working is to take a blood sample and see how long your blood takes to clot.

    How long will I take it for?

    If you’ve had a blood clot in your leg or lungs, you will probably take a short course of warfarin for 6 weeks to 6 months.

    If you take warfarin to reduce your risk of having a blood clot in future or because you keep getting blood clots, it’s likely your treatment will be for longer than 6 months, maybe even for the rest of your life.

    Is it safe to take it for a long time?

    Warfarin is safe to take for a long time. There don't seem to be any lasting harmful effects from taking it for many months and years.

    Is there an antidote for warfarin?

    Vitamin K injections can be used as an antidote to warfarin.

    You can also have intravenous fluids through a drip. Or you can take a medicine that makes your blood clot more quickly.

    What will happen if I stop taking it?

    Do not stop taking warfarin without talking to your doctor.

    If you stop taking warfarin, the rate at which your blood clots will quickly return to what it was before you started taking it. This means that you may be at increased risk of serious problems like strokes, heart attacks, DVT or pulmonary embolism.

    Are there any other similar medicines?

    Warfarin is one of the oldest and most commonly used anticoagulants.

    There are newer anticoagulants such as rivaroxaban, dabigatran, apixaban and edoxaban.

    Warfarin doesn’t seem to be any better or worse than newer anticoagulants at treating and preventing blood clots.

    However, the newer anticoagulants are more convenient because you don’t need to have regular blood tests.

    Some people take low dose aspirin to reduce their risk of heart problems. Aspirin makes your blood clot more slowly, but it works in a different way to warfarin.

    Aspirin is as good as warfarin for some health problems, but not as good for others.

    Not all medicines that prevent blood clots are suitable for everyone. Talk to your doctor or pharmacist to find out why you've been prescribed a particular medicine.

    Can I drink alcohol with it?

    You can drink alcohol while taking warfarin, so long as you stick to the national guidelines of 14 units a week for men and women. One unit of alcohol is roughly equal to a third of a pint of beer or a small glass (125ml) of wine.

    Drinking heavily - especially binge drinking lots of alcohol in one go - is dangerous while taking warfarin. The alcohol can increase warfarin’s effect and make you more likely to get bleeding.

    Is there any food or drink I need to avoid?

    The most important thing is to keep your diet stable. This means your dose of warfarin is more likely to stay the same.

    Any big changes in what you eat or drink can change how your body responds to warfarin. Speak to your doctor or nurse before changing what you eat - for example before you go on a diet to lose weight.

    Foods with a lot of vitamin K in can interfere with how warfarin works. They include green leafy vegetables, chickpeas, liver, egg yolks, cereals containing wheat bran and oats, mature cheese, blue cheese, avocado and olive oil.

    It’s important that you eat foods containing vitamin K, so rather than leaving them out of your diet, make sure you eat the same amount of them regularly. This will mean the level of vitamin K in your blood stays fairly constant, and makes it more likely your INR level stays stable.

    Do not drink cranberry juice while you’re taking warfarin. It may increase the effect of warfarin.

    Will I need to stop warfarin before surgery?

    Because of the risk of bleeding, your dose of warfarin may need to be lowered or stopped a few days before you have an operation.

    Tell the doctor that you're taking warfarin.

    Can I have a tattoo or piercing?

    It's not usually recommended to have a body piercing or tattoo while you're taking warfarin because of the increased risk of bleeding and infection.

    If you still want go ahead, talk to your doctor about your specific risks in case you need antibiotics or adjustments to your warfarin dosage.

    And let the tattooist or piercer know beforehand that you're taking a blood thinning medicine.

    Will I need to stop warfarin before dental treatment?

    You can still go to your dentist as usual. But tell your dentist if you're taking warfarin, before you have any treatment - especially if you need to have a tooth out. You may need to have blood tests before starting treatment to make sure your blood will clot enough to stop any bleeding.

    Your dentist may suggest that you stop your warfarin for 2 to 3 days before you have your tooth out. However, some people don't need to change their warfarin dose and there should be no risk of complications afterwards.

    Your dentist may contact your doctor for advice before starting the dental treatment.

    Can I have vaccinations?

    It's safe for you to have vaccinations but tell the nurse or doctor that you're taking warfarin.

    If the vaccine is normally given by injection into the muscle, then providing you're up to date with your INR tests, and the results are within the target range, you can have the injection as normal into muscle.

    Or, the injection can be given into the layer of fat underneath your skin. This is called a subcutaneous injection.

    It might help to prevent bruising if you press on the injection site for 10 minutes after the vaccination.

    Will it affect my contraception or fertility?

    Warfarin doesn't affect any type of contraception, including the contraceptive pill and the morning after pill. However, you should have your INR checked when you start or stop taking contraception.

    Can I take recreational drugs with it?

    It's thought that cannabis can increase the INR and your risk of serious bleeding.

    It's not clear how other recreational drugs like cocaine and heroin affect warfarin. They could be potentially dangerous.

    Talk to your doctor if you think you might use recreational drugs while taking warfarin.

    Do I need to avoid sports?

    You can play sports while taking warfarin, but because of the risk of bleeding it’s best to avoid ones where you’re more likely to have an injury, such as:

    • contact sports such as football, rugby, cricket and hockey
    • martial arts and kickboxing
    • horse riding

    You can carry on with non-contact sports, such as running, athletics, cycling and racquet sports. But make sure you wear protective clothing, such as a cycle helmet.

    Can I drive or ride a bike?

    Warfarin doesn’t affect your concentration so you can drive or ride a bike while you’re taking it.

    Can lifestyle changes help?

    You can reduce your risk of having a blood clot by making changes to your lifestyle, such as:

    • not smoking
    • eating a healthy, balanced diet
    • getting regular exercise
    • keeping to a healthy weight or losing weight if you need to
